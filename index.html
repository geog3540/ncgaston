Geographic Visualization GEOG:3540

Oregon Homeowner % by County

In the conext of home ownership on a county wide level, we can see that the percentages of each class do not vary widely. I chose to use natural breaks as my classification method due to this relative closeness in %. This allows us to better see which counties have a relatively lower home owner %. I chose three classes because this allows for a "low, medium, high" comparison at a glance and does not make it difficult to distinguish between classes. Not surprisingly, we see the lowest percentages in the counties with the highest population density as property prices and taxes are higher in these areas. Additionally, with more young people living there as well, this would drive down hown ownership. With that being said, it might be more interesting to view the state on a census tract level instead of county level given the size discrepancy of some of the counties.

Proportional/Graduated Symbol Mapping

There were far more wildfires within Oregon than expected. The vast majority of which are quite small (5 acres or less), while a few are very large in comparison. Since the values can vary so widely, a proportional scale was used so that all values could still be visible on the map. Interestingly, we see a larger collection of big fires with almost no smaller ones in the south east part of the state, which could merit some further investigation. Our data includes further data such as precise year and fire cause which could provide further analysis on each fire and overall trends.

Bivariate Mapping

I thought it would be interesting to see if population density throughout Oregon would affect the overall % of foreign born citizens. Since we technically have nine classes represented, frequent reference to the legend is necessary which makes the legend style useful in this case. I thought I would try rotating the legend onto its other point to see how it felt in relation to the original orientation. Finally, the choice of different color schemes is useful to swap between so as to highlight the bivariate style.
